# S01E13 — Narration (Voiceover)

Spoken-word narration voiceover for [[S01E13 — Field Notes]], written for ElevenLabs
text-to-speech to play as background narration over a slideshow / motion-comic of the five
panels (see [[S01E13 — Panel Prompts]]). Written for the ear. Dry case-file narrator — the
same voice family as prior episodes. This is the series' quietest, warmest issue: the team's
first human ally walks up on a drone-free street, hands over a hand-drawn notebook, and
leaves. The warmth lives in the content, not in the delivery — the narrator stays flat.
~225 words, ~95 seconds at 150 wpm.

Note: E13 has two lines of **dialogue** — the Junior Engineer's "The 'four' was always a
count." (Panel 3) and Tiebi's "He'll keep watching." (Panel 5) — both on-page speech
balloons, NOT the narration track; the narrator conveys the surrounding facts in his own
voice and does not re-voice the balloons. Panel 4 carries NO on-page captions (the chart and
the three bodies carry it) — but the voiceover may narrate the lineage over that beat. The
in-art PENCIL lettering on the chart (the AEGIS column labels, "PEOPLE WHO WALKED OUT", "The
'4' was a count", the red "GD-STANDARD-7" mark, the "tab 7" note) and the GD-Standard-15
caption are on-page lettering — the narrator may convey the notebook's meaning but does not
read the chart's labels as a list. No SFX fires (no powers are used).

## Clean script (paste into ElevenLabs)

> Everything in this block is spoken text only — no labels, nothing that shouldn't be
> voiced. Paste as-is.

The drone net did not cover this block. He had checked. He had been checking for eighteen months.

He had been at the facility the day it happened. He recognized Tiebi's face — and that was the only reason any of this was possible.

He crossed the street and put a notebook into Tiebi's hands. Eighteen months of work, in pencil, on the backs of old schematics. He had decided long ago what he would say. He said one true thing, and let the notebook say the rest.

AEGIS-one had fractured, and scattered. AEGIS-two had fractured, and scattered. AEGIS-three had fractured, and been recalled, and been reintegrated.

The four in AEGIS-four was the fourth time someone had needed a count.

He had crossed out the word the corporation used for two of them — non-specification outputs — and written, in his own hand: people who walked out.

Tiebi held it. Pho-boman looked once, and went still. Windman could not read it; he turned, instead, toward the place in the air where the fourth of them was not.

Then the man left. He did not wait to be thanked. He turned, and the crowd took him, and the city did not mark his going.

The drone net did not register a human face. Per revised Standard 15, this was not an exception requiring escalation.

The clock was under forty-eight hours.

## Runtime

- **Word count:** ~225 words.
- **Runtime:** ~90 s at 150 wpm; ~96 s at 140 wpm. With the tuned pauses — the long beat
  after "people who walked out." and the held lineage cascade — the track plays nearer
  **~100–105 s**. This is the slowest-paced narration of the run; let the quiet sit.
- "people who walked out." carries the episode's longest mid-track break. It is the warmest
  beat in the series so far. Deliver it flat and let the silence hold it. Do not warm the voice.
- "The clock was under forty-eight hours." is the last line. The closing break is absent by
  design — the track ends on the clock, the pause over, the rescue ahead.

## ElevenLabs delivery notes

- **Voice character:** the same dry, mid-to-low documentary narrator as [[S01E01 — Narration]]
  through [[S01E12 — Narration]] — deadpan, unhurried, flat affect. E13 is the warmest issue,
  but the warmth is entirely in the content; the voice does not change.
- **The lineage cascade ("fractured, and scattered" ×3):** even, measured, the three beats
  the same shape and weight, building only by repetition — then the fourth line ("the fourth
  time someone had needed a count") lands flat, the weight in the plainness, not a punch.
- **"people who walked out." — the warmest beat:** the Junior Engineer's humanity, in his own
  hand. Deliver it flat and small and let it sit. It is **not** a swell. The contrast — a
  cold corporate term crossed out, four human words written above it — lives in the words.
- **"the place in the air where the fourth of them was not":** Windman's absence-read,
  carried over from E10/E12. Quiet, no drama — a blind man turning toward a gap.
- **"He did not wait to be thanked. … the city did not mark his going.":** the character's
  whole register in two sentences — care that refuses to perform itself, and a city that does
  not notice. Dry, unsentimental.
- **"Per revised Standard 15, this was not an exception requiring escalation.":** the
  bureaucratic citation for why a human face does not trigger the net — institutional dry,
  the series' way of naming the blind spot without dramatizing it.
- **What not to voice:** the two on-page speech balloons — the Junior Engineer's "The 'four'
  was always a count." (P3) and Tiebi's "He'll keep watching." (P5); the in-art pencil
  lettering on the chart (the AEGIS labels, "PEOPLE WHO WALKED OUT", "The '4' was a count",
  the red "GD-STANDARD-7" and "tab 7" marks); and the GD-Standard-15 caption as written on
  the page. The narrator conveys the notebook's meaning in his own words; he does not read
  the chart's labels aloud. No SFX fires in this episode.
- **Pacing:** the two slowest moments are "people who walked out." (Panel 4 — the warmth) and
  the final clock line (Panel 5). Let both sit. Everything else moves at the series' standard
  unhurried pace — slightly slower overall here, because the episode is still.
- **Settings:** expressiveness ~30–40%, stability ~70%. Deadpan is the performance.
- **Voice archetypes to audition:** match the voice chosen for prior episodes — "George,"
  "Harry," "Arnold," or series-established equivalent. The narrator is one person across the
  series. Avoid enthusiastic / warm / alarmed / dramatic — even here, especially here.
