# S01E13 — Field Notes

> **Logline:** The only human who watched them be born walks up on a street, hands over a notebook, and tells them the number four was always a count.

---

## Continuity / placement

**Movement II.** Follows [[S01E12 — Thermal Gradient]] (Pho-boman confirmed Primm's location; 72-hour transfer clock set). Precedes [[S01E14 — Dead Reckoning]] (the rescue). By this episode's close the clock is under 48 hours.

**E12 closed** on Pho-boman walking back out of GastroDefense HQ with intelligence and a deadline — the rescue now has a target (sublevel 3) and a hard window. The three free members know where Primm is; they do not yet know what they are.

E13 arrives between the intelligence-gathering and the rescue: a pause, low-kinetic, outside the building, outside HQ surveillance range, where something the heroes could not have found by infiltrating any terminal finds them instead. The Junior Engineer does not wait to be looked for. He walks up.

E14 opens immediately from this episode's close — the clock is running, the notebook is in hand, and the rescue begins.

> **Seam test for any future edit:** does E13 open with three heroes (Windman, Tiebi, Pho-boman) in a city location outside GastroDefense HQ drone coverage, with Primm still held and the 72-hour clock already running, and does it close with the four unit codes confirmed as a lineage count rather than a model designation, the older signal planted but unexplained, and the Junior Engineer departing without ceremony? If Primm appears free, if the drone net registers the Junior Engineer, or if the older signal is decoded or explained on-page, the E13–E14 seam is broken.

## Format

This episode is a **5-panel comic page** (one page; five panels is the correct budget — this is a handoff issue, not an action issue; it earns its stillness). The kinetic register of E12 is behind it; the ensemble action of E14 is ahead. E13 is the quiet moment in between: one human, one notebook, five panels.

The visual space is a **city street, midday, exterior** — not institutional, not underground, not a GastroDefense facility. This is the first episode since the diaspora issues to be set entirely in ordinary urban space with no corporate building in frame. Daylight. Foot traffic in the background, distant. The heroes are standing still for once, in public, not hiding — because the Junior Engineer walked up to Tiebi on the street and the most neutral response was to stand still and listen.

The **notebook** is the episode's primary object, as the truck was E08's primary object and the recall specification document was E09's primary object. It is physical, pencil-annotated, held in two hands, handed across. The handoff is the issue's single kinetic moment.

Captions are the dry case-file narrator throughout. Dialogue is minimal — three lines across the whole issue, and two of them belong to the Junior Engineer, who has had eighteen months to prepare what he wants to say and chose a street. The third belongs to Tiebi. The others say nothing on-page.

### Panel 1 — The Approach

The three free heroes are in the frame, not in any particular formation — they are between places, moving, or about to move; the scout (Pho-boman) has been back from the HQ infiltration for some hours and the decision to attempt the rescue is already made. Tiebi is slightly ahead of the other two, beret straight, mustard-gold shirt catching the midday light. He is not looking at anything in particular — he is the one with a face, the one who moves through public space as a recognizable human shape, the one the world approaches when it wants to approach this group.

Windman is at his left, fan folded, pressure ghost-lines extending at low ambient range — the crowd-reading register, not the targeting register. He has catalogued the block: foot traffic mass-profiles, vehicle weights, building facades, one human mass approaching at walking pace from twelve o'clock, unaccompanied, no accelerated approach, no drone signatures overhead. He has noted it. He has not flagged it.

In the far foreground, entering the frame from the bottom edge: the top of a head. Messy light-brown hair. Round glasses. The Junior Engineer, approaching. He has been walking since he spotted Tiebi from across the block. He has not hurried. He has the notebook under his arm.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *The drone net did not cover this block.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had checked.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had been checking for eighteen months.*

**Why it lands:** The opening caption gives the audience the reason the Junior Engineer chose this location before they understand who is walking into the frame. By the time the top of his head appears at the bottom of the panel, the audience already knows he is not a threat, not a GastroDefense agent, not a coincidence — he is someone who has been finding gaps in the net and memorizing them. The heroes are mid-motion, ordinary in the street, not hiding; Tiebi is the legible human face. Windman's pressure-read noting one approaching human at walking pace, no alarm, is the minimal true confirmation that this is not an ambush. The drone-free sky above the block is the episode's visual thesis: the humans who chose the heroes find them in the spaces the net cannot see.

### Panel 2 — Recognition

**Image:** Medium shot, ground level. The Junior Engineer has reached Tiebi and stopped. They are facing each other on the pavement, the foot traffic of the street behind and around them at a remove — the pedestrians in the background not pausing, not registering anything unusual.

The Junior Engineer looks exactly as he did in E01: messy light-brown hair, round glasses, earnest focused expression — but the lab coat is gone, replaced by ordinary civilian clothes, a jacket with the ID badge lanyard tucked inside the collar. He is holding the notebook with both hands, pressed against his chest, slightly clutched. Eighteen months of something in his face: not fear, not relief — the specific expression of a person who has been carrying information they were not sure they would ever be able to deliver, and has just reached the delivery point.

Tiebi is looking at him. Tiebi's expression is the controlled-neutral of someone who has been a soldier and a security officer and knows how to assess a person at a glance: this person is not a threat. This person has seen something. This person is not affiliated with anyone who is looking for them. Tiebi's hand is at his side, not raised, not guarded. He is waiting.

Windman is slightly behind Tiebi's left shoulder, fan still folded. His pressure ghost-lines are reading the Junior Engineer at close range now: one human-mass, weight consistent, elevated respiration (the walk across the block, the eighteen months), nothing else — no concealed equipment mass, no electronic signature, no second mass behind him. The field bracket, small in the panel margin in the house format:

*[FIELD READ: single human-mass (elevated respiration) — unaccompanied — no device mass above 450g]*

Pho-boman has drifted slightly to the right and forward — drawn by something in the Junior Engineer's expression that his noodle-brow vocabulary does not have a category for yet. Noodle-brows: the interested-but-cautious configuration.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *He had been at the facility on the day of the incident.*
- CAPTION (below): *He recognized Tiebi's face.*
- CAPTION (below): *This was the only reason this was possible.*

**Why it lands:** The recognition is the structural hinge the outline ratified: the Junior Engineer can reach the team because he has a human face and walks up on a street, and Tiebi is the one hero who can be recognized as a person by another person. The caption names it plainly — he recognized Tiebi's face — without sentimentalizing it. The FIELD READ bracket in the panel margin confirms, in Windman's register, that the Junior Engineer is simply a human with a notebook: no threat, no concealment, no second party. The episode's trust is established before anyone speaks. Pho-boman's noodle-brow shift — not alarmed, not dismissive, something adjacent to the recognition-that-isn't-recognition — is the first visual signal that this exchange matters.

The moment of transfer: both sets of hands visible, the notebook between them, the Junior Engineer's grip just releasing. The notebook is physical and worn — A5 black hardcover, corners soft with eighteen months of being carried, the spine slightly cracked. Pages visible at the edge: dense pencil notations, hand-drawn diagrams, the edge of a page with a grid sketch barely readable.

The Junior Engineer is saying something. His expression is not dramatic — this is the face of someone explaining something they have checked thoroughly, not someone making a revelation. He has had eighteen months to decide what to say. He chose one sentence.

Tiebi is holding the notebook, looking at it, then back at the Junior Engineer. His other hand is at his side. He will say one thing in response, later. For now he is listening.

Windman is not in the tight frame — present but outside this close shot, the pressure ghost-lines registering at the edge. He is not reading the book; he cannot. He is reading the moment.

Pho-boman is also outside the close shot, but his noodle-brows are visible at the panel's right edge: lifted, active.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- JUNIOR ENGINEER (flat, not a reveal — delivered as a fact he verified): *The "four" was always a count.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had verified this.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had been verifying it since the incident.*

**Why it lands:** The logline's key beat lands in the mouth of the person who earned the right to say it — not the narrator, not a document, not a system update. The Junior Engineer says the sentence because he checked it, not because he is performing it. "The 'four' was always a count" in his voice, delivered flat, is the driest possible vehicle for the twist-seed the arc has been building toward: before the heroes can process what it means, they are holding the object that means it. The notebook is not a prop — it is the evidence the episode is made of. The caption "He had verified this" is the series' narrator confirming the source without elaborating on the content: the notebook holds the proof; the episode does not need to reproduce it all. The handoff is the kinetic moment of the episode and it is quiet.

### Panel 4 — What the Notebook Contains

**Image:** The notebook is open, held by Tiebi, angled so the reader can see the inside front cover. The spread is visible to the reader; Windman cannot see it (he is present at the left edge of frame, head at the correct angle for someone listening to a description rather than reading); Pho-boman's broth-lit face is reflected faintly in the open page, noodle-brows in their maximum-attention configuration.

The inside front cover shows the Junior Engineer's hand-drawn lineage chart — pencil, slightly worn, revised multiple times (earlier entries in fainter pencil, overwritten). Four columns:

*AEGIS-1 | AEGIS-2 | AEGIS-3 | AEGIS-4*

Under each column, in the Junior Engineer's cramped handwriting: incident dates, gap years, fragment-count notations. The AEGIS-1 column has two entries and a note: *2 / SCATTERED*. The AEGIS-2 column has three entries: *3 / STATUS UNKNOWN*. The AEGIS-3 column has entries, one line circled in pencil: *reintegration — completed (ref. facility log 3-C)*, and below it, a struck-through entry with a GD-Standard-7 notation. The AEGIS-4 column has four rows: W-01, P-03 (listed as SPECIFICATION OUTPUT), P-02, T-04 (listed with a question mark — the Junior Engineer had the unit codes from incident logs but not the full classification; the question mark is next to the disposal notation he could not confirm).

At the bottom of the chart, underlined twice: *The "4" was a count.*

In the margin of the same page, circled in red pencil, a note: *[see tab 7] — signal still active — [grid ref] — don't know what this means yet.*

The rest of the page: dense pencil annotations, more columns, equipment diagrams, a salvaged equipment reference number. The notebook is full. The hero reading it does not have time to read all of it here.

The Junior Engineer is in the panel's background, slightly off-center — not hovering, not waiting for a response. He is already oriented toward departure.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *AEGIS-1 fractured.*
- CAPTION (below): *AEGIS-2 fractured.*
- CAPTION (below): *AEGIS-3 fractured, and was recalled, and was reintegrated.*
- CAPTION (below, smallest): *The four in AEGIS-4 was the fourth time someone had needed a count.*

**Why it lands:** The cascade of three parallel captions — "fractured / fractured / fractured, recalled, reintegrated" — delivers the lineage without a diagram being reproduced in full. The reader sees enough of the notebook's inside-front-cover to understand the architecture; the captions deliver the narrative content of what is on the page without requiring legibility of every pencil annotation. The weight lands in the fourth caption: "The four in AEGIS-4 was the fourth time someone had needed a count." Not a twist detonation — a resonance. The prior AEGIS-3 entry with its GD-Standard-7 notation is visible in the chart but not decoded here; the reader who has been tracking the series holds that implication without having it named. The red-circled margin note — the older signal, tab 7, a grid reference — is present on the page and visible to the reader, but the caption does not visit it. That thread waits.

Above and slightly behind the three heroes, in the far background of the street, barely visible: a tab of red-pencil notation visible at the edge of the closed notebook's pages where it was marked. Tab 7. The grid reference is too small to read at this distance. The episode does not point at it.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- TIEBI *(flat)*: *He'll keep watching.*
- CAPTION: *The drone net did not register a human face.*
- CAPTION (below): *This was, per revised GD-Standard-15, not an exception requiring escalation.*

**Why it lands:** The Junior Engineer's departure is not a dramatic exit — he turns around and walks into the crowd and the crowd takes him. Tiebi's line — "He'll keep watching" — is not sentiment; it is operational assessment in three words, delivered flat, to no one in particular, because it is a true thing and Tiebi says true things. The FIELD READ not appearing here (no bracket in this panel) is the correct omission: the departure does not need to be read by Windman's pressure-sense; it has already been read and filed. The GD-Standard-15 caption — the bureaucratic citation for why the Junior Engineer does not trigger an escalation, delivered in institutional dry register — is the series' sharpest way to name the net's blind spot: not a special power, not a clever disguise, simply the fact that GD-Standard-15 does not have a category for a human face walking away from an unmarked city block on a Tuesday. Tab 7 visible at the notebook's edge in the background is the planted signal: the red-pencil marker is in the frame; the episode does not point at it; the audience holds it.

## What the audience knows that the heroes don't

- **The older signal is not background noise.** The red-pencil notation at tab 7 of the notebook — a grid reference, a signal notation, barely visible in Panel 5 — is an address: a disused former GastroDefense facility in the city where something the Junior Engineer's instruments registered but could not identify is still active. He logged the location and the reading and wrote beside it, in his own handwriting, that he does not yet know what it means. The heroes do not know either. When they follow the address — in E16 — the archive they find still running in that building will tell them two things at once: that disposal has been executed before, under GD-Standard-7, and that the truck cargo from E08 was the reagent for their own reintegration. The audience cannot yet hold all of this — but the signal exists, and the notebook contains its address.
- **AEGIS-3's reintegration was completed.** The chart's AEGIS-3 column, visible in Panel 4, shows "reintegration — completed" and a GD-Standard-7 notation beside a struck-through entry — the Junior Engineer's own pencil shorthand, copied from incident logs, not a decoded conclusion. The heroes see this but have no context for GD-Standard-7 beyond what E09's recall specification named: it covers "disposal per Product Liability / Unscheduled Outputs." Neither they nor the Junior Engineer yet know that the struck-through entry is the prior instance of Pho-boman's situation — a non-specification output disposed of under the same standard. The audience holds that implication without having it confirmed. It is confirmed in E16, from the building's archive, not from the notebook.
- **The drone net has no entry for the Junior Engineer.** GD-Standard-15 covers asset-recovery escalation thresholds for anomalous signatures. A human face walking an unmarked block does not meet the flag criteria. This is not a gap in the net — it is the net working as designed. The net was built to find AEGIS-4 unit signatures. The Junior Engineer is not one.
- **The clock.** The heroes know the clock is under 48 hours. The audience has held the 72-hour countdown since E12. This is the last pause before the rescue.

## Differentiation / what's new

Every prior encounter with information in this arc has been mediated by a corporate system: a terminal, a document, a drone feed, a status update. The heroes have read their situation off screens and specifications, always in the corporation's language, always in passive voice, always in a font.

This is the first time information arrives in pencil, by hand, from a human who chose to bring it.

The Junior Engineer is the first human in the series who *acts on their behalf* — not a bystander (the lobby crowd, the maintenance worker), not a force of nature (the Lunch Lady), not an institutional system. A person who looked at what happened, spent eighteen months cross-referencing it, and walked across a city block on a Tuesday to hand the result to the one hero with a recognizable face. The series has been building toward a "person who knows" since E01 Panel 2 (the Junior Engineer at the bench, mid-whisper to the headless chassis). This is where that character completes its first arc.

What is new here:
- **The first human ally on the heroes' side.** The distinction from every other human in the series — indifferent, oblivious, or institutional — is delivered by his presence, not announced by the narrator.
- **The lineage chart as a visual document.** The notebook's inside-front-cover is the episode's primary information object, doing the work the recall specification document did in E09 — but handwritten, in pencil, by a person who cared about the thing he built.
- **The older signal planted without explanation.** Tab 7's red-pencil notation is in the frame in Panel 4 and visible at the notebook edge in Panel 5. The episode does not point at it. It will matter in E16. The audience holds its position.
- **The minimal-dialogue episode.** Three lines of spoken text across five panels — the Junior Engineer's one sentence, Tiebi's three words. Everything else is caption, or the notebook, or the city not commenting. The episode earns its quietness by what the notebook contains.
- **The AEGIS-3 reintegration line.** Visible in the chart, not decoded. The series drops the prior disposal notation where the reader can see it without lighting it up. The heroes do not have the context yet. E16 provides the context.

## Reserved / open threads

- **The older signal (tab 7):** a still-active reading at a city address, noted in the notebook's tab 7 with the Junior Engineer's own annotation that he does not yet know what it means. The heroes have the notebook; they have not yet followed that address. This thread runs to E16, where following the address leads them to the disused facility and the archive inside it; the signal itself — later understood as a still-warm AEGIS-3 fragment in the building's walls — is a beyond-20 seed. They register it in E16; they do not act on it on-page; it remains in the city at the season's end.
- **The un-make question:** brought one step closer to full articulation by the lineage chart — others fractured before them, one prior reintegration was completed, one prior non-specification output was disposed of. The heroes now know they are not the first. The moral weight of this is not addressed on-page. The narrator does not editorialize. The question remains open through E20 and beyond.
- **The Junior Engineer's ongoing watch:** Tiebi says he'll keep watching. He will — the arc confirms his recurring role. The address his notebook logs is what sends the four to the building where the E16 decode happens; he goes quiet as the net tightens. His further appearances are beyond this episode's scope.
- **AEGIS-3's disposal notation:** visible in Panel 4's chart as a struck-through entry with a GD-Standard-7 citation in the Junior Engineer's hand. Not decoded here — by him or by the heroes. They lack the context to read it as the precedent for Pho-boman's situation. E16 provides the context, from the archive in the building, not from the notebook.
