# S01E02 — Narration (Voiceover)

Spoken-word narration voiceover for [[S01E02 — Feel the Room]], written for ElevenLabs
text-to-speech to play as background narration over a slideshow / motion-comic of the
six images (Panels 1–4 + Panel 5 beats A/B — see [[S01E02 — Panel Prompts]]) and the
print [[S01E02 — Captions]]. Written for the ear. Dry documentary-narrator voice — the
same family as [[S01E01 — Narration]] but **more intimate and lonelier**: one POV, one
man, alone the whole page. ~250 words, ~100 seconds at 150 wpm. Canon-accurate to the
seam-fixed script (2026-06-15).

> **Status:** **Author-accepted canon — 2026-06-15.** Reviewed and accepted by the
> author alongside the rest of the S01E02 set. Logged in [[Canon & Sources]].

## Clean script (paste into ElevenLabs)

> Everything in this block is spoken text only — no labels, nothing that shouldn't be
> voiced. Paste as-is.

He had been awake for about four minutes.

He didn't know what that meant. He didn't know where he was — a rooftop, three blocks from a building he'd been thrown out of and wouldn't have known by name. The bandage over his eyes had finished forming somewhere in the air, on the way down.

So he panicked. And the air panicked with him — gravel skidding, a pigeon launched straight off the roof, deeply offended. He had no idea the bird was there.

Except — for half a second — he had. When the air came back, he felt it. The wall. The gap to the next building. The bird, a moment before it flew.

So he stopped trying to see.

And the city was already there. Not as light. As pressure. The ledge. The street, six floors down. An old air-conditioner, tipping off a sill across the gap.

He didn't reach for where he heard it fall. He reached for where the air said it was going. One clean stroke, and it stopped — held in the wind like a breath, above a sidewalk that never looked up.

Somewhere across the street, a voice started a question. The city didn't let it finish.

He stood there a while, reading a city he would never see, in a language he'd just understood was his.

He had a word for it now.

Windman.

Then he came down off the roof and walked — one direction, away from the building, not looking back.

Four minutes old. Already gone.

## Runtime

- **Word count:** ~250 words.
- **Runtime:** ~100 s at 150 wpm; ~107 s at a more deliberate 140 wpm. With the tuned
  pauses and the two silent landings ("So he stopped trying to see." and "Windman.") it
  plays nearer **~105–110 s** — a touch longer and slower than S01E01's ~87 s, **by
  design**: this is the lonely, quiet issue, so it gets more air.
- If you need it under 90 s, cut the Panel-1 middle sentence ("He didn't know where he
  was…by name.") and the "deeply offended" aside — both are texture, not load-bearing.

## ElevenLabs delivery notes

- **Voice character:** same dry, mid-to-low documentary narrator as [[S01E01 — Narration]]
  — but **closer and quieter**, sitting just behind the man's shoulder rather than over
  the whole building. Flat affect that lets something through only twice: on "So he
  stopped trying to see." and on "He had a word for it now. Windman." Keep continuity by
  auditioning the **same voice** used for S01E01.
- **Pacing:** slower than E01. The Panel-3 pressure lines ("Not as light. As pressure.
  The ledge…") should be read like someone reading braille — one discovery at a time,
  unhurried. Let the long breaks be real silence.
- **"Windman." is a quiet click, not a reveal.** No trumpet, no lift in the voice — he
  arrives at it alone, almost under his breath. (The print caption's open question —
  bare *"Windman."* vs. em-dash *"— Windman."* — doesn't affect the audio; it's spoken
  softly either way.)
- **Settings:** marginally slower than conversational; deadpan is the performance. If
  available, expressiveness ~30–40 %, stability ~70 %. The prose carries the feeling, not
  the voice.
- **Voice archetypes to audition:** the dry documentary / low-affect narrator family
  ("George," "Harry," "Arnold" or similar) — match whatever was chosen for S01E01 so the
  show's narrator stays one person. Avoid enthusiastic / warm / heroic / young-energetic.
