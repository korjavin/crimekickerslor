# S01E10 — Narration (Voiceover)

Spoken-word narration voiceover for [[S01E10 — Half-Life]], written for ElevenLabs
text-to-speech to play as background narration over a slideshow / motion-comic of the five
panels (see [[S01E10 — Panel Prompts]]). Written for the ear. Dry case-file narrator — the
same voice family as prior episodes. This is the series midpoint: the first time the team
loses. The narrator runs two registers in one flat tone — the corporation's workflow (a
department resuming function, a clock, a confirmation filed) and the heroes' consequence
(one captured, three out, a gap in the air) — and does not change voice to signal which
side it is on. ~195 words, ~80 seconds at 150 wpm.

Note: E10 has **no dialogue** — GastroDefense does not speak, and the heroes have no lines
this issue. Only narration. Two SFX fire as visual lettering (STOMP and HWUF in Panel 4);
they are lettering, not narration. The corporate-screen and tablet text visible in the
panels — the Panel 1 status tablet and its ticker (including *1 NULL*), the drone
correlation logs (Panels 2 and 4), the *T-04 — SIGNATURE STATUS: UNCONFIRMED* tablet
(Panel 4), and the Panel 5 hold-notice tablet and the *ASSET RECOVERED 1/4* ticket update —
are in-panel institutional lettering; do not voice them as narration.

## Clean script (paste into ElevenLabs)

> Everything in this block is spoken text only — no labels, nothing that shouldn't be
> voiced. Paste as-is.

The Retrieval Division had not processed a ticket since the third quarter of the previous fiscal year.

It was now processing one.

A crew, a cage, and a clock. Nine minutes out. None of the four knew it.

The van was following a route. The route was a protocol. The protocol was a form.

One of them drifted toward the lot at the end of the block. There was metal under a tarp, and he wanted to know how much.

The structure was rated for standard equipment.

No one had written a specification with him in mind. No one had considered him.

A worker closed the door. The latch was on the outside. This was within spec.

The other three left the way each of them was built to.

One set his beret outside the gap before he went through it. A beret does not fit through eight centimeters. He does.

One simply walked. The net had been calibrated for a signature he did not carry, so it did not see him go.

One went up — into the column of air the sweep did not cover — and filed the exit where he could reach it.

The protocol had a field for three confirmed signatures. It did not have a field for what happened to them next.

Transport was on hold. The procedure required both halves of the original body, and the division had one. So it would wait.

Behind them, in the air he reads instead of sees, something that had always answered did not.

He had no word for this.

He filed it where he could reach it.

## Runtime

- **Word count:** ~210 words.
- **Runtime:** ~85 s at 150 wpm; ~90 s at 140 wpm. With the tuned pauses — the long
  two-second silence after "No one had considered him." and the held beat on "So it would
  wait." — the track plays nearer **~95–100 s**.
- "No one had considered him." carries the episode's longest break by design. The silence
  is the capture landing — not as cruelty, as indifference. Do not shorten it.
- "He filed it where he could reach it." is the last line. The closing break is absent by
  design — the track ends on that phrase without air after it, the same dry filing-gesture
  the episode closes the midpoint on.

## ElevenLabs delivery notes

- **Voice character:** the same dry, mid-to-low documentary narrator as [[S01E01 — Narration]]
  through [[S01E09 — Narration]] — deadpan, unhurried, flat affect. The two registers
  (corporation / heroes) are handled in one narrator voice; do not shift register or tone
  to signal which side of the turn is being narrated. The dryness is uniform. The audience
  distinguishes context by the content, not the voice.
- **"This was within spec." — the rubber stamp:** delivered flat and small, exactly as in
  E09. The capture is filed, not dramatized. Do not make it sinister; it is a worker closing
  a door and a confirmation going into a system.
- **"No one had considered him." — the still center:** this is the episode's coldest beat.
  Deliver it flat and unhurried, then let the long break sit. It is not an accusation. It is
  an accurate description of a corporation that built the prototype and never imagined it
  would become a person. The narrator does not editorialize.
- **Panel 4 — the three exits:** the comedy is in the flatness, not in performance. The
  beret line ("A beret does not fit through eight centimeters. He does.") is funniest read
  completely straight, no wink. Same for the walk-out — the joke is that nothing happened.
- **"He had no word for this." — the loss:** the midpoint's emotional landing. Deliver it
  flat. It is **not grief** — it is a being processing an experience his vocabulary does not
  yet have. The contrast lives in the words and the silence around them, not in the voice.
- **What not to voice:** all in-panel institutional lettering must NOT be narrated — the
  Panel 1 status tablet and its ticker (including *1 NULL* and *ETA 9 MIN*), the drone
  correlation logs (Panels 2 and 4), the *T-04 — SIGNATURE STATUS: UNCONFIRMED* tablet
  (Panel 4), the Panel 5 hold-notice tablet text and the *ASSET RECOVERED 1/4* ticket
  update. The STOMP and HWUF SFX in Panel 4 are visual lettering and carry no narrated
  sound. None of these appear in the clean script above.
- **Pacing across the episode:** the two slowest moments are the break after "No one had
  considered him." (Panel 3 — the capture) and the close on "He filed it where he could
  reach it." (Panel 5 — the loss). Let both sit. Everything else moves at the series'
  standard unhurried pace.
- **Settings:** expressiveness ~30–40%, stability ~70%. Deadpan is the performance.
- **Voice archetypes to audition:** match whatever voice was chosen for prior episodes —
  "George," "Harry," "Arnold," or series-established equivalent. The narrator is one person
  across the series. Avoid enthusiastic / warm / alarmed / dramatic.
