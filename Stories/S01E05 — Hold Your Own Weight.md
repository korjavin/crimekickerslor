# S01E05 — Hold Your Own Weight

> **Logline:** A purple ninja phases through every floor of a corporate tower in
> freefall, looks completely composed doing it, and eventually figures out that the
> problem was never the phasing.

---

## Continuity / seam

This episode is a **zoom-in on [[S01E01 — Some Assembly Required]] Panel 4**, bottom-left
quadrant of the wake-up grid. That panel's verbatim descriptor is the episode's opening
image (Panel 1 here): *"a purple-coated figure halfway through a corridor wall. Gasping,
phasing free. The suit is fully formed, cyan visor glowing, twin blades sheathed on his
back, mini-orbs already orbiting his waist."* The episode opens exactly here, mid-phase,
one foot still inside the wall.

The episode's **final panel** delivers Primm to [[S01E01 — Some Assembly Required]]
Panel 5's overhead compass-spoke shot: four figures walking outward from the GastroDefense
Inc. campus on four streets. His figure is rendered there as *"the purple coat descending
a stairwell to the street."* This episode must close with him in motion, descending a
stairwell toward street level — not already outside, not stationary, and not meeting anyone.

> **Seam test for any future edit:** does E01 Panel 5's overhead shot read as the
> immediate next frame after E05 Panel 5? If Primm reaches the street *within* this
> episode, or if the stairwell is replaced with any other exit route, the seam breaks.

---

## Format

This episode is a **5-panel comic page** (one page). Each panel is one image with tight
captions / minimal dialogue. Iconography over blocking.

The episode is **vertical** — where E03 (Tiebi) crossed four horizontal spaces and E02
(Windman) stayed on a rooftop, Primm falls *down* through the GastroDefense tower, one
floor per panel, phasing through every surface he tries to grip. The joke the audience
reads is: he looks exactly like a finished superhero and he cannot stay on a floor. The
episode has no wide exteriors until the final panel's stairwell. Every image is tight,
close, slightly vertiginous — beams of fluorescent light sliding past, dropped ceiling
tiles, service conduit, the institutional architecture of a building being passed through
*involuntarily*.

Primm is alone throughout all five panels. No teammate appears. No named human is present
— one pair of legs at extreme background distance in Panel 3, oblivious, already gone by
the time the panel resolves. This is intentional: the diaspora rule holds. He is not
discovered. He is not helped. He figures this out alone, in a stairwell, beside a primer can.

> **Visual production:** image-generation prompts for each panel to be built against the
> [[Visual Style Guide]] by the panel-prompt-smith agent.

## Working title

**"Hold Your Own Weight"** — the eureka stated as the episode's thesis. He has been
nullifying his own mass without knowing it; the answer is to turn it back on and let
the floor catch him. The title doubles as a dry piece of personal advice he didn't know
he needed.

Alternates: "The Dial He Was Already Turning" · "Floor Optional" · "DRIFT" · "Perfectly
Controlled Descent" · "You Can't Grip a Wall You Weigh Nothing"

---

## Cast (this issue)

- **The unnamed fragment** — no name until Panel 5. One of the three AEGIS-4 fragments;
  his equipment crystallized at split-time. He is minutes old and has no human past, no
  prior framework, no memories. He is a blank slate inside a very complete-looking costume.
  **Appearance:** slim, lean, ~175–180 cm; slightly elongated face, sharp focused eyes,
  short-to-medium dark brown hair; high-tech goggles with faintly blue-tinted lenses
  visible behind the cyan-glowing visor; sleek lab-coat-style jacket, dark purple with
  silver trim, open at the front; black undershirt with faint glowing blue geometric lines;
  dark blue fitted trousers tucked into black boots with silver accents; small metallic
  mini-orbs orbiting chest and waist, glowing faint blue; black gloves with silver
  fingertips; katanas (ninjato-style straight blades) sheathed flat across his back.
  Gravity-use VFX: space ripples faintly around him, light bends slightly at arm's reach.
  Teleport VFX: faint glowing blue trails.
- **A corridor wall** — the first surface he was inside. Present Panel 1 only. No lines.
- **Successive floors, railings, and dropped ceilings** — the building offers each of
  these to him in turn and he passes through all of them. Present Panels 1–4. No lines.
- **A primer can** — a dented metal paint-primer can, label reading PRIMM, kicked to one
  side of a stairwell landing during some prior maintenance job and left there, slightly
  rusted at the base, entirely unremarkable. The name-bearing object. Present Panel 5 only.
  No lines.

## Panels

### Panel 1 — Halfway Out

**Image:** This is the verbatim continuation of [[S01E01 — Some Assembly Required]] Panel 4
(bottom-left quadrant). A GastroDefense Inc. service corridor — beige walls, grey linoleum,
a fluorescent strip above — seen from a slightly low angle, as if the camera is at chest
height looking down the hallway. At the center of the frame: a purple-coated figure
emerging from a solid wall. He is already three-quarters free — both arms are out, hands
open, the left leg has cleared the wall, the right foot still dissolving through the plaster.
The transition is visible: at the boundary where he exits the wall, the outline of his body
is semi-transparent, bluish-ghosted, the silver trim on his jacket flickering slightly. The
cyan visor glows. The mini-orbs orbit. The twin blades are sheathed correctly on his back.
The suit is complete. He looks exactly right. His expression — visible through the visor's
glow, readable from the slight forward angle of his head — is the expression of someone who
did not decide to do this. His open hands are reaching for the corridor floor reflexively,
as if to catch himself. They will pass through it. He doesn't know that yet.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION (dry, boxed, case-file register): *He had been awake for approximately one second.*
- CAPTION (below, same register): *He was already somewhere he had not chosen to be.*
- CAPTION (below): *Both of these things were going to remain true for a while.*

**Why it lands:** The continuation of E01 P4 bottom-left is immediate and unambiguous —
same figure, same wall, same suit, same halfway position the origin issue captured. The
captions echo the series formula ("approximately one second" — parallel to E04's "no
minutes," E02's "four minutes," E03's "six minutes") and establish the dry case-file voice.
The important visual detail is the open hands: he is reaching downward for a floor that
his own mass-nullification field will let him fall straight through. The audience who can
read gravity can see what is coming. He cannot. He looks, from the outside, like a hero
making a controlled entrance.

---

### Panel 2 — Every Floor Has an Opinion

**Image:** A dizzying vertical composite — not a split panel but a single image rendered as
a falling-through-ceilings progression, the camera locked on Primm while the building
scrolls *up* around him. He is mid-fall through what is clearly the second or third floor
below the corridor: the architecture moves in bands — a dropped office ceiling (acoustic
tiles, the ghostly suggestion of a conference table below), then structural concrete, then
the open air of a server room with rack lighting, then concrete again. He passes through
all of it. Each band has the same faint semi-transparent ghost-effect at the boundaries
where his body intersects a surface. His arms are extended — three times in the image, at
three successive floor-levels, his gloved hands have reached for something to catch:
a ceiling grid rail, a railing that doesn't exist at this level, the edge of a raised
platform. All three reach-gestures phase directly through the surface they targeted.
The blue trails of a half-attempted blink flicker mid-image and dissolve without completing —
he tried to teleport sideways and couldn't commit the destination. He is still falling. His
posture is not panicked. His posture is focused, increasingly narrow-eyed behind the visor,
the expression of someone running a diagnosis and not yet having a result. The mini-orbs
orbit slightly erratically — some trailing wide, caught in the turbulence of descent.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *He reached for the ceiling rail.*
- CAPTION (below): *The ceiling rail was not interested.*
- CAPTION (below): *He tried the railing.*
- CAPTION (below): *The railing had the same opinion.*
- CAPTION (below): *The floor below that was completely consistent in its views.*

**Why it lands:** The five-caption staircase delivers the accumulation of failures at
maximum deadpan compression — each item in the list is a new floor, a new refusal, and
the last caption (naming not the railing or the rail but "the floor below that," as if
the building is offering a verdict rather than a surface) tips the rhythm from slapstick
into the building having opinions. The vertical composite image is the episode's most
dynamic — but it is dynamic in a downward direction only, and Primm is still perfectly
composed in silhouette throughout. The incomplete blink-trail is the first evidence that
he has tried something active and been defeated by it. The erratic orbs are the one visual
cue that something underneath the cool exterior is not running correctly. He does not look
frightened. He looks like a superhero falling through four floors and finding it
professionally unsatisfying.

---

### Panel 3 — Wrong Dial

**Image:** A wide utility space — a sub-floor service level, concrete, low ceiling, exposed
conduit, a fire-rated door to one side standing ajar. The bottom of the fall, for now: he
has landed on something — a heavy steel cable tray bolted near the floor, the kind that
runs electrical conduit along a wall. It is the first surface thick enough and dense enough
with metal that his field did not entirely nullify contact with it. His boots are resting on
it — not standing on it, resting, the way a balloon rests against a ceiling: he is not
gripping the floor with his weight, he is merely hovering against the cable tray with one
boot, held by the faintest of incidental friction. One hand is out for balance. His other
hand is raised in front of the cyan visor. He is looking at his own hand. The look is
diagnostic, narrow-eyed, completely still: the look of someone who has identified that
the problem is *themselves* and is working out precisely which part. The faint light-bend
of gravity manipulation is visible around his hand and forearm — the air thickens slightly
at arm's reach, the kind of optical distortion that says *mass is not behaving correctly
here*. At the very edge of frame, in the background, a pair of boots is visible (a building
maintenance worker, transit only) walking away through the fire-rated door, already
disappearing. The worker did not notice him. The worker is already gone.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- SFX (subtle, integrated into the light-bend distortion around his hand):
  *DRIFT—*
- CAPTION: *He had been trying to stop phasing.*
- CAPTION (below): *He was not, it turned out, in control of the phasing.*
- CAPTION (below): *Something else had been on.*
- CAPTION (below): *Something else had been on the whole time.*

**Why it lands:** Panel 3 is the diagnostic beat — the moment the diagnosis narrows from
"phasing is wrong" to "something under phasing is wrong." The SFX DRIFT lands here as the
canonical first deployment: it belongs not to the teleport or the phase but to the gravity
field itself, the ambient hum of mass-nullification running at full tilt on his own body.
It is a quiet SFX — not a crash or a HWUF or a SLURP, but a drift, a shift, a thing
happening without drama. The four-caption staircase (two matched pairs: "He had been
trying / He was not in control" then "Something else had been on / the whole time") is the
eureka beginning to form. He has not named it yet. He has located where to look. The
maintenance worker leaving through the fire door is the episode's only other human presence
— and they do not look back, do not notice, do not register him at all. He is alone in the
sub-floor, hovering against a cable tray, running the calculation.

---

### Panel 4 — Turn It On

**Image:** The same sub-floor service level, ten seconds later. A single image of one
decision, captured in the instant before and the instant after. **Before (left, slightly
desaturated, lighter weight):** Primm crouched low, eyes closed behind the visor,
completely still, the light-bend of the gravity field visible around his whole body —
the air thickening in a radius around him, a visible distortion that signals mass is
being held off. He is not touching the floor; his boots hover a centimeter above the
concrete. **After (right, full color, grounded):** the same crouch, heavier — both boots
flat on the concrete, a small impact-scatter of fine grit around each sole, the light-bend
distortion *gone* from around his body (still present faintly at arm's reach, but no
longer enveloping him). His eyes are open. The cyan visor is bright. The mini-orbs have
snapped into tight, disciplined orbit — no drift, no scatter. His hands are at his sides,
gloved fingertips slightly spread, the posture of someone who has just confirmed a
hypothesis and is choosing to remain very still about it. He is standing on a floor. The
floor is holding him. He did not phase through it. The cable tray beside him no longer
matters. The fire door to his left is the route forward.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *He stopped trying to grab the world.*
- CAPTION (below): *He turned his own weight back on.*
- CAPTION (below): *The floor caught him.*
- CAPTION (below): *It turned out floors do that.*

**Why it lands:** The eureka is physical and visual, not verbal. The before/after
technique (parallel to E03 Panel 5's ghost-image) makes the resolution a single frame
rather than two panels — efficient, iconographic. The four-caption staircase delivers
the thesis at maximum compression: the first two lines are the action, the third is the
result, the fourth is the deadpan observation that strips any sense of triumph from the
moment. Floors catch things. That is what floors are for. He just needed to weigh something.
The mini-orbs snapping to tight orbit is the visual signature of him being in control for
the first time — they were drifting in Panel 2, they were slightly scattered in Panel 3,
and now they are exactly where they belong. The light-bend field pulling back to arm's reach
(rather than enveloping his body) signals the switch: he is not nullifying his own mass
anymore, only managing the field outward. He is standing. He has never stood on a floor
before and meant it.

### Panel 5 — PRIMM

**Image:** A fire-exit stairwell in the GastroDefense Inc. tower — concrete steps, metal
handrail (painted red, institutional), the narrow vertical shaft of a building staircase
going down. Seen from a medium angle, slightly above and behind him: Primm is descending
the stairs. He is in motion — one boot on a step, the other about to land on the one
below it, the purple coat moving with him, the mini-orbs in tight orbit, the cyan visor
bright, the twin blades still sheathed and steady on his back. He is walking, not running.
He is not looking up at the floors he fell through. He is looking down, toward the street,
toward the light at the base of the stairwell, toward the compass-spoke exit that is
waiting below. His posture is exact: the slight forward lean of controlled descent, weight
fully committed. He looks like a hero who just arrived on purpose. On the landing between
floors — just at the lower left corner of the frame, easy to miss if you're watching him —
a dented metal paint-primer can sits against the wall where a maintenance worker left it
and forgot about it. The can's label, slightly faded, reads: **PRIMM. UNDERCOAT FOR METAL
SURFACES. APPLY BEFORE PAINTING.** One corner of the label has peeled up.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION (bottom right, smallest and most offhand line in the issue, dry-corporate
  register): *Primm.*

**Why it lands:** The name arrives in the stairwell as a maintenance artifact — a
paint-primer can, left behind by an unknown worker, entirely unconnected to him. PRIMM
UNDERCOAT FOR METAL SURFACES: the thing applied before anything else sticks, before the
real coat lands. He does not see it. He does not need to. He is already walking. The
caption names what the environment has already named, in the dry case-file voice that has
named all four this way: not by revelation, not by ceremony, not by anyone saying it aloud
— just by observing what is already true. The peeled-up label corner rhymes with E04's
peeled masking-tape PHO label on the cauldron: the diaspora arc's names are always
slightly worn, slightly accidental, sitting in institutional spaces nobody is looking at.
His posture is the episode's final punchline and its final beat: he is descending the
stairwell with the perfect composure of a man who has always known how to do this. He has
not always known how to do this. He worked it out six floors ago, alone, beside a cable
tray, with nobody watching. The audience knows. He will not mention it. The next wide
frame a reader encounters — [[S01E01 — Some Assembly Required]] Panel 5's overhead shot,
"the purple coat descending a stairwell to the street" — is the immediate next image: he
is walking toward it right now.

## What the audience knows that the hero doesn't

- He is one of three fragments from Project AEGIS-4; one more being was born separately
  from the incident. He has no idea any of the others exist.
- The dial he found — weight, mass, his own gravitational commitment to a surface — is the
  same dial that makes objects within his reach lose 80% of their weight. He has found it
  on his own body first. He has not yet discovered what it does at arm's length. That is
  the power that will matter later. This is the prologue to it.
- He looked like a finished superhero when he phased through the corridor wall at Panel 1.
  He also looked like a finished superhero while falling through four floors. He looked
  exactly like a finished superhero when he figured it out. The audience has been watching a
  being with the aesthetics of total competence discover that competence is something you
  build. He has built, so far, one floor's worth.
- GastroDefense Inc. considers him "Recalled." He is descending a stairwell in their own
  building and they do not know he is here.
- His vulnerability to large metal masses is canon but untested in this issue. The cable
  tray caught him incidentally — the first hint of the mechanic — but he did not register
  it as significant. Something in his future is going to be very, very metal.
- He will never tell anyone about the falling. He will never tell anyone about the primer
  can. These are private facts, and he is, constitutionally, not going to bring them up.

## Tone targets

- **Panel 1:** controlled, slightly surreal, the comedy tucked inside the image. He looks
  entirely right; that is the joke. The corridor is institutional-beige; the only vivid
  color is the cyan visor-glow and the faint purple of the coat. The opening captions are
  dry and flat, the voice of a system describing its own situation without editorializing.
- **Panel 2:** the episode's most kinetic panel, but the kinesis is vertical and
  accumulative. Slapstick by architecture — the building has consistent opinions and he
  is consistently wrong about them. Five short captions land five floors of failure in one
  image. The incomplete blink-trail is the only moment of active defeat.
- **Panel 3:** the pivot. Slower beat. One held image of him looking at his own hand. The
  DRIFT SFX lands quiet — not an explosion, a process. Four captions shift the diagnosis
  from "phasing" to "something else." The maintenance worker leaving through the fire door
  is the episode's one non-character human, used correctly: they do not notice him, they do
  not comment, they leave. He is alone with the problem.
- **Panel 4:** before/after resolution. The ghost-image technique reads fast. The four
  captions are the eureka at compression. The final caption ("it turned out floors do that")
  deflates any sense of triumph. He is standing on a floor. This is a floor. He is not
  going to make a speech about it.
- **Panel 5:** composed, forward-moving, quiet. The cool three-point ninja energy is fully
  present — he is descending stairs and he looks like he was born knowing how. The primer
  can is easy to miss and that is correct. The dry single-line caption is the episode's last
  and smallest word. He does not look back.

## Differentiation from E02, E03, and E04

- **E02 (Windman):** Contemplative, sensory, rooftop-still, quiet-melancholy. Power
  discovery is gradual, graceful, an unfolding of perception. He earns the name alone on
  a parapet as a quiet internal arrival. The register is lonely-meditative.
- **E03 (Tiebi):** Mobile, slapstick, physics-puzzle, populated, four horizontal spaces.
  Power discovery is a kinetic eureka after a sequence of architectural failures, building
  logically from closet to revolving door to parking ramp. He earns the name from a parking
  stall marker. The register is comic-mechanical.
- **E04 (Pho-boman):** Still, existential, cosmically warm. The comedy is the complete
  absence of any framework for existence — he is not confused about his power, he is
  confused about everything at the same level. The power fires involuntarily at a fluorescent
  tube. He earns the name from a catering label he does not look at. The register is
  bewilderment-as-contentment.
- **E05 (Primm):** Vertical, vertiginous, the comedy of a being who looks like a complete
  hero and cannot stay on a floor. The power discovery is a *reversal* — he believed the
  problem was phasing (the power he is visibly using), and the answer is gravity (the power
  he has been running invisibly on himself the whole time). The eureka is the opposite of
  reaching outward: he stops fighting the world and commits his own weight to it. He earns
  the name from a maintenance primer can he does not see. The register is **competence
  achieved accidentally, performed as though it was always the plan.** He ends on
  coolness — the first of the four to land in a posture that reads as genuinely capable —
  and the audience knows he did not mean to. He will never admit it.

The four diaspora episodes form a deliberate tonal square: Windman is still/sensory,
Tiebi is mobile/physical, Pho-boman is still/existential, Primm is mobile/strategic.
E05 closes Act II on the note of *apparent* competence, which is the correct setup
for Act III's reunion arc — where four strangers who each believe they have figured
something out will have to figure out something together.
