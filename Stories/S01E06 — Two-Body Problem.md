# S01E06 — Two-Body Problem

> **Logline:** A blind man who feels everything and a man who can be any size run into each other on a night street, accidentally prevent a mugging together, and both fail to walk away from it.

---

## Title

**"Two-Body Problem"** — the physics term for two interacting masses whose relationship cannot be solved by treating either in isolation. Here it is also the emotional fact: the first time either of them has been in the same problem as another person, and the problem cannot be solved alone. It rhymes with E03's momentum-physics flavor and Tiebi's physics-first way of understanding himself. The title is dry enough to sit in the "What Is a Ladle For?" / "Momentum Not Included" family.

---

## Continuity / placement

This is **Issue 6, opening Act III.** It follows [[S01E05 — Hold Your Own Weight]] (Primm's issue, Act II close) and precedes the second reunion issue. Neither Pho-boman nor Primm appear here.

Windman closed E02 at street level, walking one compass-spoke direction away from the GastroDefense campus, fan folded, not looking back. By E06, time has passed — he is somewhere in the city, no fixed address, drifting. The episode does not need to account for every day between E02 and E06; it opens on him already present in a specific place at a specific time.

Tiebi closed E03 on the top deck of the parking garage, walking toward the compass-spoke street, not looking back. By E06, same logic applies — time has passed, he has been moving.

This episode does not close into any seam from E01 (both characters' E01 handoffs are already resolved). It is a free-standing Act III entry that opens the reunion arc and feeds forward to whatever follows in Issue 7.

> **Seam test for any future edit:** does this episode end with exactly two figures — Windman and Tiebi — walking the same direction, neither having split off or been named as a team? If a third hero appears, if the team is named, or if one of them leaves alone, the Act III opening beat is broken.

## Format

This episode is a **6-panel comic page** (one page). The extra panel over the Act II standard is justified: five panels is not enough to carry (a) the meeting mechanic, (b) the cooperative vigilante act, (c) the uncanny-familiar flicker, and (d) the inverted-ending beat without rushing any of them. Six panels is the correct budget. Each panel is one image with tight captions and, for the first time in the series, sparse direct dialogue between two heroes.

The visual space is a **night street** — one block, one incident. Where the diaspora issues occupied multiple locations (E03: four spaces) or a single contained space (E02: one rooftop), E06 is tight and specific: a narrow city block at night, a pool of streetlight, a running figure, two strangers. The darkness is the right register for Windman (he does not need light) and for Tiebi (small, able to disappear). The streetlamp is the one warm pool of color in an otherwise dark palette — a different visual logic than the diaspora issues' institutional fluorescent.

Captions are still the dry case-file narrator. Dialogue is minimal, flat, deadpan. The narrator does most of the work; the characters speak when the silence cannot do it.

## Cast (this issue)

- **Windman** — blind, 180 cm, moderately stout, chestnut hair. Blue-and-red eye bandage (top blue, lower red). Blue T-shirt with large white "W" monogram. Brown knee-length shorts. Fully black flat boots. Multicolored three-blade fan (green, blue, red blades on a 25 cm brown handle), carried in the right hand. His perception: the city as a pressure-map — every displaced surface, every moving mass, every breathing body registers as a faint push or pull in the air around him. He/him.
- **Tiebi** — size-shifter, ~175–180 cm at full size, medium build, slightly lean. Dark-navy French beret, slightly tilted, small nub at top center. Mustard-gold fitted shirt. Plain dark trousers and flat dark shoes. Can shrink below a cockroach, grow larger than an elephant; size and momentum are independent dials. Low-ceiling vulnerability (irrelevant outdoors). He/him.
- **The runner** — one figure, fast, holding something taken. Not a named villain, not a face — a body in motion, hooded, leaving fast. Present Panels 3–5. No name. No lines.
- **The person left behind** — barely in frame, stumbling to a halt as the runner leaves. Not focused on; not heroic. Present Panel 3 only, background.
- **A streetlamp** — the one warm pool of light on an otherwise dark block. The stage. No lines.

## Panels

### Panel 1 — Pressure Map, Singular

**Image:** Night. A city block seen from above and to the side — buildings, parked cars, a single streetlamp casting its yellow pool on the pavement. The block is quiet. In the middle of the frame, standing still in the shadow between two parked cars — not under the lamp, deliberately not — is Windman. Fan is open in his right hand, blades spread, not aimed at anything. His bandaged face is slightly turned away from the viewer, chin lifted at the angle of someone who is not looking but listening with the whole body. The city registers around him in the ghost-line pressure-map register established in E02 Panel 3: the parked cars as dense mass-shadows, the building walls as hard reflective planes, a cat on a distant ledge as a tiny warm blip. The block is otherwise empty. He is perfectly at ease in the dark.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION (dry, boxed, case-file register): *He had been in this city for several weeks.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had mapped a significant portion of it in air.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had not yet found anything that felt like himself.*

**Why it lands:** This re-establishes Windman's perception register from E02 for any reader coming in from the diaspora arc, and plants the emotional thesis of the issue in the final caption. "Found anything that felt like himself" is deliberately ambiguous — the pressure-sense reads the city as a continuous signal, and in that whole signal there has been no anomaly. He is the only impossible thing in his own field. The ghost-line overlay is the visual callback to E02 Panel 3; the reader who has read E02 sees it immediately. The darkness is correct: his world is built in pressure, not light, so night is not a disadvantage. He is more comfortable here than in any previous panel because he is operating in the condition that suits his perception exactly.

---

### Panel 2 — Wrong Shape In The Air

**Image:** Same block, same angle. On the far end of the block — at the edge of the streetlamp's pool — Tiebi is walking. Full size, dark-navy beret, mustard-gold shirt visible even in the low light. Normal human pace. He is not aware of Windman, who is still in the shadow between the parked cars. He is looking at the street ahead of him the way someone looks at a street they are crossing for no particular reason. What the panel also captures: in the ghost-line pressure overlay, there is something *wrong* with Tiebi's silhouette. The air around him reads as slightly off — not the dense mass-shadow of a parked car, not the warm blip of a normal body. When Tiebi shifts size even fractionally (a micro-adjustment, unconscious, the body calibrating), the pressure field around him collapses and expands in a way that does not match anything Windman has mapped before. The ghost-lines around Tiebi's figure flutter — not random noise, but a *shape that doesn't hold*. In the foreground, between the cars, Windman's head has turned. Fan still open. He has stopped doing anything else.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *The city had a new signal.*
- CAPTION (below): *It read like a person. It read like something that did not stay one size.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had not mapped anything like it.*
- CAPTION (below): *He did not know what it meant.*
- CAPTION (below): *He kept tracking it.*

**Why it lands:** This is the meeting mechanic, and it is delivered before they speak or even before Tiebi knows Windman exists. The pressure-sense catches the size-flutter — the tiny unconscious micro-calibrations Tiebi's body makes without his thinking about it. Tiebi is not doing anything deliberately; his body just does not hold a fixed mass signature the way a normal body would. To Windman this is an anomaly unlike anything in weeks of mapping. The "did not stay one size" caption is the clearest statement of what his sense is detecting, in terms a reader can parse. The emotional beat in the last two captions is quiet: he does not know what it means, but he does not look away. That is new. The fan being open in the foreground signals that something has activated without him deciding it.

---

### Panel 3 — Incident

**Image:** The streetlamp's pool, bright and sudden. A runner — hooded, moving fast — has cleared a stumbling figure in the background and is now heading down the center of the block toward the viewer, holding something small and taken (a bag strap, a phone, unclear). The stumbling figure is in the far background, one hand out for balance, not giving chase. The runner is fast. In the foreground, Windman is already moving toward the street — not running, pivoting, fan swinging back in preparation. On the far side of the block, Tiebi has stopped. He has seen the runner. He has not seen Windman yet. He has, however, noticed the trajectory of the runner and is already doing the physics: he is looking at the gap between two parked cars at the block's far end, calculating. Both of them have seen the same thing. Neither of them knows the other one has seen it.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *The anomaly stopped mattering for approximately four seconds.*
- CAPTION (below): *Someone was running who should not have been.*
- CAPTION (below): *Independently, both of them decided this was their problem.*

**Why it lands:** The "independently" is the key word — it is not altruism framed heroically, it is just two beings who have spent weeks alone noticing the same thing at the same time. The city that has been an empty pressure-map for Windman and a series of physics problems for Tiebi is suddenly presenting a shared object. The caption "The anomaly stopped mattering for approximately four seconds" is the right dry comedy note: Windman was fascinated by Tiebi and then dropped it immediately when the actual event happened. The stumbling figure in the background is present and then gone — not focused on, not turned into a speaking victim. The incident exists as context for what the two of them do, not as its own narrative.

---

### Panel 4 — Sensor and Actuator

**Image:** A wide-angle of the block mid-action. The runner is still in motion, now center-frame — but now there are two things happening simultaneously and the runner does not know about either. On the right side: Windman's fan completes a deliberate lateral sweep — not a panic-blast but a controlled, aimed compression wave (concentric pressure-rings in the ghost-line register, rolling across the pavement) that catches the runner mid-stride from the side. Not a strike: a shove, a wobble, a stagger. The runner's momentum breaks — one foot stutters, the trajectory bends, he slows. On the left side: Tiebi has gone small — the mustard-gold smear of him at micro-scale, a postage-stamp runner already at full sprint in the gap between two parked cars, dark-navy beret a moving blur. He is sprinting micro, carrying the momentum of a full-sized start-up into a tiny frame. The concentric pressure-rings from Windman's gust are visible across the panel, as is the tiny smear of Tiebi's micro-sprint. Both are present in the same image. They have not spoken to each other.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- CAPTION: *The blind man felt the runner's pace and weight.*
- CAPTION (below): *He aimed at where the runner would be in two seconds.*
- CAPTION (below): *He had no idea anyone was sprinting in the gap between the cars.*
- CAPTION (below): *The man in the gap had no idea the compression wave was coming.*
- CAPTION (below): *They were, accidentally, covering each other's angles.*

**Why it lands:** This is the cooperation moment, and it is framed as accidental — neither one is acting on the other's presence, and yet they are already forming a sensor-actuator pair. Windman uses his pressure-read to aim ahead of the runner (not at him, at where he will be in two seconds — this is new, it is the perception and the power being used simultaneously the way E02 Panel 4 fused them, but now deployed in the field). Tiebi is doing E03's eureka: sprint small to build momentum, grow big to carry it. He is sprinting now, the grow coming in Panel 5. The captions doing double-take ("no idea anyone was sprinting… no idea the wave was coming") is the dry comedy, but it also does the thematic work: two people in the same problem, independent, accidentally complementary. The ghost-line register is very dense in this panel — two separate actions visible, the pressure-rings from the gust and the micro-smear of Tiebi's sprint. The runner does not know either of them is there.

---

### Panel 5 — Sprint First, Then Grow

**Image:** The runner has staggered from the gust and is now bracing — he has caught himself, he is not down, but he has lost a full second. In the gap to his left, at the precise moment his foot re-plants for the recovery stride: Tiebi, at full size, mid-forward-roll, arriving at exactly the point the stagger has made available. This is E03 Panel 5's method paid forward — the sprint's momentum is now the full-size body's momentum, and the roll carries him directly into the runner's path as a suddenly human-sized obstacle. The runner has no way to avoid it. The roll does not knock the runner down clean — this is not a clean combat move — but it catches the runner's legs and brings both of them down, the bag or phone or whatever-it-was skidding across the pavement. Tiebi ends up sitting on the ground, slightly winded, beret somehow still on. The SFX of the arrival. In the background, Windman is walking toward the lamp's pool from the shadows, fan folded shut, at a pace that suggests he knows exactly where the scene is without looking at it.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- SFX (integrated into the arrival, the exact same energy as E03 Panel 5's eureka — not a new technique, the same one, now deployed in the world): *STOMP—*
- CAPTION: *The runner stopped.*
- CAPTION (below): *Nobody was more surprised than the man who stopped him.*

**Why it lands:** The STOMP SFX is Tiebi's canonical SFX from the character file, and it lands here not as a macro-form ground-pound but as the arrival after a micro-sprint — the same physics from E03 but in a real-world context, paying off the origin issue's lesson. "Nobody was more surprised than the man who stopped him" is the right comedy note: this worked, but Tiebi did not plan it this cleanly, and the beret being somehow still on his head is the visual confirmation that his style holds even when he's sitting on the pavement. The runner is down but not injured — this is street-level, small-scale, grounded. Windman walking toward the scene from shadow with a folded fan is the visual punchline: he has not run, has not changed his pace, has navigated by pressure to exactly the right spot. He already knew where it would end.

---

### Panel 6 — Two-Body Problem

**Image:** The aftermath. The block is quiet again. The runner is gone (someone else's problem now, off-panel). The bag or phone has presumably been left behind, presumably recovered by the stumbling person who has also already cleared the frame. What remains: the streetlamp's pool on the pavement, and two figures standing at its edge. Windman, fan folded in his right hand, not aimed anywhere. Tiebi, beret straightened (back to its proper tilt), brushing grit off his trousers. They are standing a half-step apart and both are facing the same direction — outward, down the street, away from where the runner came from. Neither of them is looking at the other. The overhead register — the same one from E03 Panel 6 and E01 Panel 5 — frames them: two figures, one street, same direction.

This is the first time in the entire series that any panel has ended with two of them in the same frame.

**Caption / dialogue:**
- TIEBI (flat, not a speech): *You can't even see me.*
- TIEBI (beat): *You can't even SEE.*
- WINDMAN (equally flat, not a speech): *You changed size twice before I found you.*
- *(a pause in the captions)*
- CAPTION (dry, offhand, smallest and most offhand line in the issue): *Neither of them said the word "team."*
- CAPTION (below, same register): *They walked the same direction anyway.*

**Why it lands:** The first real dialogue in the series between two heroes is not a declaration or a recognition or a dramatic reveal — it is Tiebi pointing out the absurdity of being tracked by a blind man, and Windman noting, without expression, that he clocked Tiebi's size-changes two panels before the incident happened. "You changed size twice before I found you" is the reveal that the meeting mechanic was not a coincidence: Windman had already located Tiebi as an anomaly. The uncanny-familiar flicker is in that line — he does not explain why Tiebi read the way he did, and neither of them goes further than this. The "wait, do I know you?" undertone lives in what neither of them says after that exchange. The dry narrator line doing the ending — "neither of them said the word 'team'" — is the button: it does the work of naming the thing by naming its absence, which is more accurate and more in the series' register than any positive declaration. The final image, two figures, same direction, is the visual inversion of every single-figure exit from E02 through E05. Every diaspora episode ended on one person, not looking back. This one ends on two.

## What the audience knows that the heroes don't

- Windman and Tiebi are fragments of the same cyborg. The anomaly Windman detected — the pressure signature that "did not stay one size" — is, at a level neither of them has any language for, a familiar signal. He has never felt any other fragment. He does not know what he would feel if he did. But the audience knows that the signal that felt *not alone* was, in some sense, a piece of himself.
- The words Windman would need to articulate this do not exist. He knows: it was different. That is all he knows. He has filed it under: the city changed.
- Tiebi has no idea he is one piece of something. He does not know there is a piece of him in the city that just tracked him through darkness without being able to see him.
- GastroDefense Inc. has logged all four as "Recalled." They do not know two of them are standing under a streetlamp on the same block right now.
- The cooperation that just happened — the stagger-and-roll, the aimed gust, the momentum-exit — was neither of them at full capacity. It was two powers used accidentally together. The audience can see what this becomes when it is intentional. The two of them cannot see that yet.

## Tone targets

- **Panel 1:** quiet and particular. Windman at ease in the dark, city as pressure-map. The emotional weight is in the last caption. Palette: dark blue-grey city, one distant warm lamp, the ghost-line overlay. No humor yet — this is a man who has been alone in a city for weeks.
- **Panel 2:** the tonal shift is in the ghost-lines, not in Windman's face. The anomaly registers before either of them has done anything. The comedy is the last caption — he does not know what it means, but he keeps tracking it. Low-key.
- **Panel 3:** a gear-shift. The block comes alive for four seconds. Both of them, independently, at the same moment. The "independently, both of them" caption is where the comedy picks up.
- **Panel 4:** the most kinetic panel. Two things happening at once in one frame — pressure-rings and micro-sprint. The captions are doing double-takes. This is where the slapstick-scaling-up of Act III starts, but it is grounded in the physics already established.
- **Panel 5:** the payoff of E03's eureka in the real world. Short and fast. STOMP, beret still on, Windman walking calmly from the shadow. The comedy is in the two captions' dry delivery of surprise.
- **Panel 6:** quiet again, like Panel 1, but plural. The dialogue is dry and flat. The narrator button is the driest line in the issue. The overhead register is the formal signal that something has changed. Two figures, same direction. No announcement.

## Differentiation / what's new

The four diaspora issues (E02–E05) were each a solo power-discovery: Windman's sensory unfolding, Tiebi's physics slapstick, Pho-boman's existential inventory, Primm's gravity-reversal. Each one was an episode about a person learning what they are, alone.

E06 is not that. No new power is discovered. Nothing is learned about the self. What changes is a different kind of fact: *I am not the only one.* That is the episode's subject, and it requires two people.

This is the series' first relational episode, its first real dialogue, and the first panel that ends with more than one of the four in frame at the same time. The slapstick scales up slightly from the diaspora issues — the incident is kinetic, the double-coverage is comedic in structure — but the register underneath is still the lonely-funny of the diaspora arc, now in the early process of becoming something else. Neither of them has a word for what just happened. The narrator has one word for what did not happen: "team." Saying it was absent is the same as saying it was close.

The visual inversion of the series' ending beat — every previous exit was one figure walking alone — is the structural marker that Act III has begun. The episodes that follow will build on this. This one just plants the first fact: two bodies, same problem, same direction.
